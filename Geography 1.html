1. Introduction to Geography & Geological Time Scale

Weightage: 0 + 2 Qs · Foundational chapter — sets up concepts used everywhere else

📌 What is Geography?

  • Geography = study of the Earth's surface, its physical features, climate, population, and human activity, and their spatial relationships (Greek: geo=earth, graphien=to describe).
  • Two broad branches: Physical Geography (landforms, climate, oceans) and Human Geography (population, settlements, economy).

🕰️ Geological Time Scale

  • Earth's 4.6 billion years of history is divided into Eons → Eras → Periods → Epochs (largest to smallest units).
  • Eons (oldest→newest): Hadean → Archean → Proterozoic → Phanerozoic.
  • Phanerozoic Eon (visible life) has 3 Eras: Paleozoic → Mesozoic → Cenozoic (we live in Cenozoic).
  • Mesozoic Era = "Age of Reptiles/Dinosaurs"; Cenozoic = "Age of Mammals".
EraApprox. PeriodKey Feature
Paleozoic541–252 myaFirst fish, land plants, amphibians
Mesozoic252–66 myaDinosaurs; ends with mass extinction (66 mya, asteroid impact)
Cenozoic66 mya–presentRise of mammals, humans (Quaternary Period)

🌌 Earth Formation Theories

  • Nebular Hypothesis (Kant-Laplace): Solar system formed from a rotating cloud of gas & dust (nebula) that collapsed under gravity.
  • Big Bang Theory: Universe originated ~13.8 billion years ago from a single, extremely dense, hot point that expanded rapidly — proposed by Georges Lemaître; universe is still expanding (evidence: redshift of galaxies, Edwin Hubble).
  • Accretion theory: Earth formed by gradual sticking together (accretion) of dust and gas particles orbiting the young Sun.
💡 Mnemonic — Era order "Please Make Coffee" → Paleozoic → Mesozoic → Cenozoic (old to new).

2. The Earth & The Universe

Weightage: 57 Qs — Highest-weightage World Geography chapter ⭐

☀️ The Solar System

  • Consists of the Sun (a star), 8 planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids, comets, meteoroids.
  • Order of planets from Sun: Mercury → Venus → Earth → Mars → Jupiter → Saturn → Uranus → Neptune.
  • Inner/Terrestrial planets (rocky): Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars. Outer/Jovian planets (gas/ice giants): Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.
  • Pluto reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006 by IAU — has not cleared its orbital neighbourhood. 5 recognised dwarf planets: Ceres, Pluto, Haumea, Makemake, Eris.
💡 Mnemonic — Planet order "My Very Educated Mother Just Served Us Nachos" → Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.

Sun, Stars & Galaxies

  • Sun composition: Hydrogen ~70%, Helium ~26.5%, other elements ~3.5%; diameter ~109× Earth's.
  • Sunspots: cooler (~6,500°F vs surrounding), dark patches on Sun's surface; vary in an 11-year solar cycle.
  • Solar flares / CMEs (Coronal Mass Ejections) = huge bursts of radiation/particles from Sun's corona → cause geomagnetic storms, affecting GPS, power grids, submarine cables, pipelines (via geomagnetically induced currents).
  • Aurora (Northern/Southern Lights): charged solar particles trapped by Earth's magnetic field, glowing near poles.
  • Star life cycle (Sun-like star): Nebula → Protostar → Main Sequence (most of its life; fuses H→He) → Red Giant → sheds outer layers → White Dwarf.
  • Galaxy = huge group of stars, gas & dust bound by gravity. Our galaxy = Milky Way (looks like a "milky" band of light).
  • Galaxy types: Spiral (arms, like Milky Way), Elliptical, Irregular (no defined shape, gas/dust-rich, active star formation), Lenticular.
  • Comets: icy bodies; release gas/dust tails when nearing Sun (heat causes sublimation), e.g. C/2020 F3 (NEOWISE).
  • Asteroids/Meteoroids: rocky, mostly in Asteroid Belt (Mars–Jupiter); meteoroid burning in atmosphere = meteor ("shooting star"); one reaching ground = meteorite.
  • Exoplanet: a planet orbiting a star outside our solar system.
  • 2024 PT5: a temporary "mini-moon" that briefly orbited Earth for ~2 months in 2024.

The Earth — Basics

  • 3rd planet from Sun; only known planet with life; called "Blue Planet" (71% surface = water).
  • Densest planet in the solar system; 5th largest overall; largest of the 4 terrestrial planets.
  • Shape: Geoid ("earth-like shape") — an oblate spheroid, flattened at poles, bulging at equator (due to centrifugal force + rotation). Equatorial bulge ≈ 42.72 km / 26.5 miles.
  • Average distance from Sun ≈ 149.6 million km (1 Astronomical Unit, AU).
  • Age ≈ 4.5–4.6 billion years.
  • Only 3% of Earth's water is fresh; 97% is saline.
  • Atmosphere divided into 5–6 layers (see Atmosphere chapter).
  • One natural satellite: the Moon.

3. Latitudes, Longitudes & Standard Time

Part of "The Earth & Universe" weightage — core map-skill chapter

📏 Latitudes

  • Imaginary east–west circles parallel to Equator; used to measure location north/south.
  • Range: 0° (Equator) to 90°N / 90°S.
  • Distance between two latitudes ≈ 111 km (constant everywhere).
LatitudePositionNote
Equator0°Longest latitude; divides Earth into N & S Hemispheres
Tropic of Cancer23.5° NSun directly overhead in June
Tropic of Capricorn23.5° SSun directly overhead in December
Arctic Circle66.5° NNorthernmost of the 5 major circles
Antarctic Circle66.5° SSouthernmost of the 5 major circles
  • Frigid Zones: Pole ↔ Polar circle — coldest, midnight sun & polar night.
  • Temperate Zones: Polar circle ↔ Tropic — mild, all 4 seasons.
  • Torrid Zone ("Tropics"): Tropic of Cancer ↔ Tropic of Capricorn — hottest, sun overhead seasonally.

🧭 Longitudes

  • Imaginary north–south lines (semi-circles) converging at poles; measure location east/west of Prime Meridian (0°, Greenwich).
  • 180° East + 180° West = 360° total; Prime Meridian splits Earth into Eastern & Western Hemisphere.
  • Unlike latitudes, distance between longitudes decreases towards the poles.

🕒 Longitude & Time

  • Earth rotates 360° in 24 hours → 15° of longitude = 1 hour = 4 minutes per degree.
  • International Date Line (IDL): runs along 180° longitude (curved near Bering Strait/Fiji to avoid splitting countries); crossing it changes date by exactly 1 day.
  • Indian Standard Time (IST): based on 82°30′ E longitude (passes near Mirzapur, UP) — 5 hrs 30 min ahead of GMT.
  • India's longitudinal extent (~30°) causes ~2 hour actual sunrise difference between Arunachal Pradesh & Gujarat, but a single IST is used for uniformity.
  • World has 24 standard time zones (each 15° wide). USA/Canada: 5 zones. Russia: 11 zones (historically; ~9 now used practically).
💡 Tip — Direction of date change at IDL Travelling West→East (e.g. Asia→America) across the IDL: subtract a day (go back).
Travelling East→West (e.g. America→Asia): add a day (go forward).

4. Earth's Magnetism

Weightage: 2 Qs

  • Earth behaves like a giant bar magnet, generated by convection currents in the molten outer core (iron-nickel) — called the "geodynamo" effect.
  • Magnetosphere: region around Earth dominated by its magnetic field; shields Earth from solar wind & cosmic radiation.
  • Magnetic North/South Pole ≠ Geographic North/South Pole (magnetic poles shift over time).
  • Geomagnetic storm: temporary disturbance of the magnetosphere caused by solar wind shockwaves / CMEs — can disrupt satellites, GPS, and power grids.
  • Charged solar particles trapped by the magnetic field near the poles create the Aurora Borealis (north) / Aurora Australis (south).

🌋 Origin of the Earth

  • Formed via accretion ~4.6 billion years ago from the solar nebula.
  • Early Earth was molten; heavier materials (iron, nickel) sank to form the core — process called differentiation.
  • Atmosphere & oceans formed later through volcanic outgassing and cometary/asteroid water delivery.

7. Plate Tectonics & Distribution of Oceans/Continents

Weightage: 4 Qs

🧩 Continental Drift → Plate Tectonics

  • Continental Drift Theory (Alfred Wegener, 1912): all continents were once joined as a single supercontinent, Pangaea, surrounded by ocean Panthalassa; it broke apart and drifted to current positions.
  • Pangaea first split into Laurasia (north) and Gondwanaland (south).
  • Plate Tectonic Theory (1960s): lithosphere is broken into rigid tectonic plates floating on the semi-molten asthenosphere, driven by mantle convection currents.
  • Major plates: Indian, Eurasian, Pacific, North American, South American, African, Antarctic; Minor plates: Nazca, Philippine, Cocos, Caroline, Arabian, etc.

🔀 Types of Plate Boundaries

Boundary TypeMovementResult
Divergent (Constructive)Plates move apartMid-ocean ridges, rift valleys, new crust forms (e.g. Mid-Atlantic Ridge)
Convergent (Destructive)Plates collideMountains (Himalayas), ocean trenches, subduction zones, volcanoes
TransformPlates slide past each otherNo new crust created/destroyed; major earthquakes (e.g. San Andreas Fault)
🔹 Example The Himalayas formed by the convergent collision of the Indian Plate (moving north) with the Eurasian Plate — the Indian Plate was once south of the equator and much larger (included present-day Australian plate) before splitting.
  • Nazca Plate: between South America & Pacific plate (subducts under South America — Andes formed).
  • Caroline Plate: between Philippine & Indian plates (north of New Guinea).
  • Evidence for plate tectonics: matching coastlines, fossil correlation across continents (e.g. Mesosaurus), matching rock strata, paleoclimatic evidence, seafloor spreading & magnetic striping.

8. Earthquakes, Volcanism & Tsunamis

Weightage: 4 + 3 Qs

🌐 Earthquakes

  • Focus (Hypocentre): point inside Earth where rupture originates. Epicentre: point on surface directly above the focus (experiences strongest shaking).
  • Measured by Richter Scale (magnitude, logarithmic — each whole number = 10× amplitude) and Mercalli Scale (intensity/observed damage).
  • Causes: plate tectonic movement, volcanic activity, faulting; man-made triggers: reservoir-induced seismicity, mining.
  • India's seismic zones: Zone V (highest risk, e.g. Kashmir, NE states, Kachchh) to Zone II (lowest risk).

🌋 Volcanism

  • Movement of molten magma (inside earth) / lava (after reaching surface) & associated gases through weak zones in the crust.
  • Types by activity: Active (erupting/could erupt), Dormant (inactive but may erupt again), Extinct (unlikely to erupt again).
  • Types by shape: Shield volcano (broad, gentle slopes, basaltic lava — e.g. Hawaii), Composite/Strato volcano (steep, alternating lava/ash layers, explosive — e.g. Mt. Fuji), Caldera (large collapsed crater), Mid-ocean ridge volcanism.
  • Pacific Ring of Fire: horseshoe-shaped zone around the Pacific Ocean with ~75% of world's active/dormant volcanoes and most earthquakes — due to subduction zones.
  • India's only active volcano: Barren Island (Andaman & Nicobar Islands).

🌊 Tsunamis

  • Series of massive ocean waves usually triggered by undersea earthquakes (most common), volcanic eruptions, or landslides.
  • 2004 Indian Ocean Tsunami (26 Dec 2004): triggered by a massive undersea earthquake off Sumatra; devastated Andaman & Nicobar Islands and coastal India/Sri Lanka/Thailand/Indonesia.

10. Landforms and Their Evolution

Weightage: Fluvial 3 + Arid/Water-eroded 5 + Marine 1 + Glacial 3 + Karst 3 Qs

🏞️ Fluvial Landforms (by Rivers)

StageErosional LandformsDepositional Landforms
Youthful (upper course)V-shaped valleys, gorges, waterfalls, potholes, rapids—
Mature (middle course)Meanders beginLevees, flood plains
Old age (lower course)—Meanders, ox-bow lakes, braided channels, deltas, sand bars
  • Ox-bow lake: formed when a meander loop is cut off from the main river channel.
  • Delta: triangular depositional landform at river mouth (e.g. Sunderbans — Ganga-Brahmaputra delta, world's largest).

🏜️ Arid & Water-Eroded Landforms (Deserts)

  • Shaped mainly by wind (aeolian) action & occasional flash floods.
  • Erosional: Mushroom rocks (Zeugen), Yardangs, Inselbergs, Deflation hollows/Blowouts.
  • Depositional: Sand dunes — Barchans (crescent-shaped, transverse to wind), Seif dunes (longitudinal, parallel to wind), Loess (wind-blown fine silt deposits).
  • Playa: shallow temporary desert lake, water evaporates leaving salt deposits — key inland-drainage feature.

🌊 Marine (Coastal) Landforms

  • Shaped by wave action, tides & currents.
  • Erosional: Sea cliffs, sea caves, sea arches, stacks, wave-cut platforms.
  • Depositional: Beaches, spits, bars, tombolos, lagoons.

🧊 Glacial Landforms

  • Shaped by moving ice (glaciers).
  • Erosional: Cirque (armchair-shaped hollow), U-shaped (glacial trough) valleys, Arêtes (sharp ridges), Horns (pyramidal peaks — e.g. Matterhorn), Hanging valleys.
  • Depositional: Moraines (lateral, medial, terminal, ground), Eskers, Drumlins (streamlined mounds), Outwash plains.

🕳️ Karst Topography

  • Formed by chemical weathering (solution) of soluble rocks, mainly limestone, by slightly acidic water (carbonation).
  • Name derived from the Karst region of Slovenia.
  • Surface features: Sinkholes (dolines), Karst windows, limestone pavements.
  • Underground features: Caves, Stalactites (hang from cave roof), Stalagmites (rise from cave floor), Pillars (where the two join).
  • Famous Indian karst areas: parts of Meghalaya (e.g. limestone caves near Cherrapunji/Mawsynram).
💡 Mnemonic — Stalactite vs Stalagmite StalaCtite = hangs from Ceiling (tight grip, "hold on Tight"). StalaGmite = grows from Ground.

11. Composition & Structure of Atmosphere

Weightage: 4 Qs

🌫️ Composition

  • Atmosphere = mixture of gases + water vapour + dust particles enveloping the Earth; 99% of its mass lies within 32 km of the surface.
  • Nitrogen ~78%, Oxygen ~21%, Argon ~0.93%, Carbon dioxide ~0.04% (variable, rising due to fossil fuels), plus trace gases, water vapour & dust.
  • CO₂: transparent to incoming solar (shortwave) radiation but opaque/absorbs outgoing terrestrial (longwave) radiation → main greenhouse gas.
  • Ozone (O₃): found 10–50 km up (concentrated in stratosphere); absorbs harmful UV radiation, acting as Earth's "sunscreen".
  • Water vapour: up to 4% of air by volume in humid tropics, <1% in deserts/polar regions; decreases with altitude & toward poles; found only up to ~90 km.
  • Oxygen becomes negligible above 120 km.
  • Dust particles: sea salt, soil, smoke, ash, pollen, meteor debris — act as hygroscopic nuclei for cloud/condensation.

🪜 5 Layers of the Atmosphere

LayerHeightKey Feature
Troposphere0–13 km avg (8 km poles, 18 km equator)All weather occurs here; temp falls 1°C/165m; most important for biological activity
Stratosphere13–50 kmContains the ozone layer; ideal for aircraft (stable, no turbulence)
Mesosphere50–80 kmColdest layer (down to –100°C); meteors burn up here
Thermosphere (incl. Ionosphere 80–400 km)80–400+ kmTemperature rises with height; ionosphere reflects radio waves

💡 Mnemonic — Atmosphere layers (bottom→top) "Tiny Stars Make Things Easy" → Troposphere, Stratosphere, Mesosphere, Thermosphere, Exosphere.
  • Tropopause: boundary between troposphere & stratosphere; ~ –80°C over equator, ~ –45°C over poles (nearly constant temp here).
  • Mesopause: boundary of mesosphere (upper limit).
  • Geographers mainly focus on the first two layers (troposphere & stratosphere) since these directly affect climate/weather & UV protection.

12. Winds (Planetary & Local), Temperature & Humidity

Weightage: 1 + 1 Qs

Westerlies30°–60° N/SBlow from subtropical high to sub-polar low; "Roaring Forties" in S. Hemisphere
Polar Easterlies60°–90° N/SCold, dry winds from poles
  • Caused by pressure belts (Equatorial Low, Subtropical High, Subpolar Low, Polar High) + Coriolis force (deflects winds right in N. Hemisphere, left in S. Hemisphere — due to Earth's rotation).

🌬️ Local Winds

  • Land & Sea Breeze: daily cycle — sea breeze (day, sea→land), land breeze (night, land→sea), due to differential heating/cooling of land vs water.
  • Loo: hot, dry summer wind of North Indian plains.
  • Chinook (N. America) / Foehn (Alps): warm, dry winds descending leeward mountain slopes.
  • Mistral: cold wind in southern France.

🌡️ Temperature & Humidity

  • Insolation = incoming solar radiation; varies with latitude, angle of sun's rays, day length, transparency of atmosphere.
  • Factors controlling temperature distribution: latitude, altitude, distance from sea, ocean currents, prevailing winds.
  • Humidity = amount of water vapour in air. Absolute humidity = actual water vapour content (g/m³). Relative humidity = ratio of actual to maximum possible water vapour at that temperature (%).
  • Warmer air holds more moisture → relative humidity drops as temperature rises even if absolute moisture stays same.

🌡️ Ocean Salinity & Temperature

  • Average ocean salinity ≈ 35 parts per thousand (‰); highest in Red Sea/Dead Sea (high evaporation, low inflow); lowest near river mouths & polar regions (dilution by fresh water/ice melt).
  • Ocean temperature decreases with depth & with increasing latitude.

🔄 Ocean Currents & Tides

  • Ocean currents: large-scale movement of seawater driven by wind, Earth's rotation (Coriolis), density/temperature differences. Warm currents flow from equator to poles (e.g. Gulf Stream); cold currents flow from poles to equator (e.g. Labrador Current).
  • Tides: periodic rise/fall of sea level caused mainly by gravitational pull of Moon (and Sun).
  • Spring tides: highest tides, during New/Full Moon (Sun-Earth-Moon aligned, gravitational pull adds up).
  • Neap tides: lowest tide range, during first/last quarter moon (Sun & Moon pull at right angles, partially cancel).

15. Location & Extent of India

Weightage: 9 Qs

  • India's mainland extends from Kashmir (north) to Kanniyakumari (south), and Arunachal Pradesh (east) to Gujarat (west).
  • Latitudinal extent: 6°45′ N to 37°6′ N (~30°). Longitudinal extent: 68°7′ E to 97°25′ E (~30°).
  • North–south distance ≈ 3,214 km; East–west distance ≈ 2,933 km (shorter, because longitude convergence increases towards poles — India lies in the northern/sub-tropical latitudes).
  • Total area: 3.28 million sq km = 2.4% of world's land area; 7th largest country in the world.
  • Territorial waters extend 12 nautical miles (~21.9 km) from the coast.
  • Coastline: 6,100 km (mainland), 7,517 km (including islands).
  • Standard Meridian: 82°30′ E (passes through Mirzapur, UP) → gives IST = GMT + 5:30.
  • Southern part of India lies within the Tropics; northern part in the sub-tropical/warm temperate zone → responsible for huge diversity in landforms, climate, soils & vegetation.
  • India is separated from Sri Lanka by the Gulf of Mannar & Palk Strait.
  • Neighbouring countries (land border): Pakistan, Afghanistan, China, Nepal, Bhutan, Myanmar, Bangladesh. Longest land border: Bangladesh. (Note: China shares the longest disputed/contested border, but Bangladesh has the longest official land frontier.)
  • Island neighbours: Sri Lanka & Maldives.
💡 Tip India = 7th largest country. Countries larger than India: Russia, Canada, USA, China, Brazil, Australia.

16. Physiographic Divisions of India

Weightage: 37 Qs ⭐ High-weightage chapter

🏔️ 3 Geological Divisions

  1. The Peninsular Block — ancient, rigid, stable (gneiss & granite); part of Indo-Australian Plate; oldest landmass.
  2. The Himalayas & other Peninsular Mountains — young, weak, flexible (folded); still tectonically active.
  3. Indo-Ganga-Brahmaputra Plain — a filled geo-synclinal depression; alluvium depth 1,000–2,000 m.

🗺️ 6 Physiographic Divisions (macro relief)

1. Northern & N-E Mountains 2. Northern Plain 3. Peninsular Plateau 4. Indian Desert 5. Coastal Plains 6. Islands

1. Northern & North-Eastern Mountains (Himalayas)

  • Consist of a series of parallel ranges: Greater Himalaya (Himadri), Lesser Himalaya (Himachal), and Shiwaliks (outermost, youngest).
  • Great Himalayan range length ≈ 2,500 km; width 160–400 km; orientation changes region to region (NW–SE in Kashmir; E–W in Sikkim/Darjeeling; N–S in Nagaland/Manipur/Mizoram).
  • Himalayas act as physical, climatic, drainage & cultural divide between India and Central/East Asia.

2. The Northern Plain

  • Formed by alluvium of Indus, Ganga & Brahmaputra; ~3,200 km long, 150–300 km wide; elevation 50–150 m.
  • North to south zones: Bhabar (narrow, 8–10 km, coarse gravel belt below Shiwaliks) → Tarai (10–20 km, marshy, dense vegetation) → Alluvial Plains (Bhangar = older alluvium, upland; Khadar = newer alluvium, floodplain).
  • Famous depositional feature: Sunderbans Delta (Ganga-Brahmaputra) — world's largest delta.
  • Haryana & Delhi form the water divide between Indus & Ganga systems.

3. The Peninsular Plateau

  • Rises 150 m (from river plains) to 600–900 m elevation; irregular triangle shape; one of the oldest, most stable landmasses.
  • 3 broad groups: Deccan Plateau, Central Highlands, Northeastern Plateau.

4. The Indian Desert (Marusthali)

  • NW of Aravalis; low rainfall <150 mm/year; arid climate, longitudinal dunes & barchans.

  • Main river: Luni (ephemeral); many streams end in salt lakes/playas (source of salt).

17. Drainage System (Rivers of India)

Weightage: 43 Qs ⭐⭐ Highest-weightage Indian Geography chapter

🌊 Classification

  • By sea discharge: Bay of Bengal drainage (~77% area — Ganga, Brahmaputra, Mahanadi, Krishna, Godavari, Kaveri) vs Arabian Sea drainage (~23% — Indus, Narmada, Tapi, Mahi, Periyar).
  • By origin: Himalayan drainage (perennial — fed by snowmelt + rain) vs Peninsular drainage (mostly seasonal/rain-fed, except a few like Narmada/Tapi in rift valleys).
  • By basin size: Major (>20,000 sq km, 14 basins e.g. Ganga, Brahmaputra, Krishna); Medium (2,000–20,000 sq km, 44 basins); Minor (<2,000 sq km).

🏔️ Himalayan Drainage — Evolution

  • Theory: an ancient master river "Indo-Brahma" (Shiwalik river) once flowed the entire Himalayan length (Assam→Punjab→Sind) during the Miocene (5–24 mya); later dismembered by Pleistocene tectonic uplift into 3 systems: Indus (west), Ganga (central), Brahmaputra (east).

The Indus System

  • One of the largest river basins in the world; total length 2,880 km (1,114 km in India); origin: near Bokhar Chu, Kailash range, Tibet (called "Singi Khamban"/Lion's Mouth there).
  • Key tributaries: Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, Satluj (together = "Panjnad", joining above Mithankot); flows through Ladakh & J&K in India, then Pakistan → Arabian Sea.

The Ganga System

  • Rises as Bhagirathi from Gangotri glacier (Gaumukh), Uttarkashi; meets Alaknanda at Devprayag → becomes "Ganga".
  • Alaknanda formed by Dhauliganga + Vishnuganga (meet at Joshimath/Vishnuprayag); other tributaries: Pindar (Karnaprayag), Mandakini (Rudraprayag) — the famous Panch Prayag confluences.
  • Enters plains at Haridwar; total length 2,525 km; splits into Bhagirathi & Padma distributaries in the delta.
  • Major tributaries: Yamuna, Ghaghara, Gandak, Kosi (north bank — Himalayan); Chambal, Betwa, Son (south bank — Peninsular, older than the Himalayan rivers).
  • Kosi = "Sorrow of Bihar" — notorious for frequently shifting its course due to silt deposition.
  • Namami Gange Programme (2014): flagship river conservation mission — pillars: sewage treatment, river-front development, river surface cleaning, biodiversity, afforestation.

The Brahmaputra System

  • Rises near Mansarovar Lake (Tibet, known as "Tsangpo" there); enters India in Arunachal Pradesh as the Dihang/Siang; takes a sharp turn southward at Dhubri to enter Bangladesh.
  • Known for riverine islands (e.g. Majuli — world's largest river island) & frequent floods due to braided channels.

18. Climate of India & Monsoon System

Weightage: 9 + 7 Qs

🌦️ Overview

  • India's climate = "Monsoon type" — characterised by seasonal reversal of winds.
  • 4 main seasons: Cold weather (winter) Dec–Feb, Hot weather (summer) Mar–May, South-West Monsoon (rainy) Jun–Sep, Retreating/Post-monsoon Oct–Nov.

🌬️ Mechanism of Monsoon

  • South-West Monsoon (Jun–Sep): moist winds from Arabian Sea/Bay of Bengal blow onto land due to a low-pressure system over NW India (intense summer heating) — brings ~75-80% of India's annual rainfall.
  • Splits into two branches: Arabian Sea branch (hits Western Ghats first — Kerala onset ~1 June) & Bay of Bengal branch (moves up through NE India & the Gangetic plain).
  • North-East (Retreating) Monsoon (Oct–Dec): reversed dry winds from land to sea; brings winter rainfall to Tamil Nadu & Coromandel coast (only region getting rain in this season, due to picking up moisture over Bay of Bengal).
  • Key theory: Jet Stream theory — withdrawal of the subtropical westerly jet stream from north of Himalayas triggers monsoon onset.
  • Mawsynram & Cherrapunji (Meghalaya) — wettest places on Earth (funnel-shaped topography intensifies orographic rainfall).

🌡️ Regional Variation

  • Rainfall decreases from east to west in the northern plains, and from coast to interior in the peninsula.
  • Rain-shadow region: Deccan plateau (east of Western Ghats) — much drier than Konkan coast.
  • Coldest region: Kashmir/Ladakh & Siachen; Hottest: Rajasthan (Thar Desert, Churu/Ganganagar).
  • Loo: hot, dry summer wind over North Indian plains (April–June).
  • Mango showers: pre-monsoon showers in Kerala/Karnataka that help mango ripening.
  • Kal Baisakhi ("Nor'westers"): violent pre-monsoon evening thunderstorms in West Bengal/Assam.
💡 Tip — Monsoon onset order Kerala (~1 June) → Mumbai → Kolkata → Delhi (~ end June/July) — monsoon "arrives" in India from the south and moves northward/eastward over weeks.

22. Transportation in India

Weightage: 8 Qs

🛣️ Roads

  • National Highways = only ~2% of total road length but carry ~40% of road traffic.

26. World Population — Distribution, Density & Growth

Part of Population Geography's 113 Qs — theory foundation

🌍 Distribution Pattern

  • Population is highly uneven: 90% of world population lives on just ~10% of land area.
  • Top 10 most populous countries hold ~60% of world population; 6 of these 10 are in Asia.
  • Density of Population = Population ÷ Area (persons per sq km).

📊 Factors Influencing Distribution

CategoryFavourable factors
GeographicalWater availability, flat plains/gentle slopes, comfortable climate, fertile soil
EconomicMinerals & mining, urbanisation, industrialisation (e.g. Katanga-Zambia copper belt, Kobe-Osaka Japan)
Social/CulturalReligious/cultural significance; conversely, unrest/political instability repels population

📈 Population Growth — Key Terms

  • Growth of Population: change in numbers between two points in time.
  • Natural Growth = Births − Deaths. Actual Growth = Births − Deaths + In-migration − Out-migration.
  • CBR (Crude Birth Rate) = (Live Births ÷ Population) × 1000. CDR (Crude Death Rate) = (Deaths ÷ Population) × 1000.
  • Positive growth: births > deaths, or net in-migration. Negative growth: deaths > births, or net out-migration.

  • Thomas Malthus (1798): population grows faster than food supply → unchecked growth ends in famine/disease/war ("Malthusian catastrophe"); preventive checks (family planning) better than "positive checks" (famine, war).
  • India is currently transitioning between Stage II and III — mortality has fallen sharply, fertility declining but not yet at replacement level everywhere.

🚶 Migration

  • Place of Origin (population decreases) vs Place of Destination (population increases).
  • Immigrant = moves INTO a place; Emigrant = moves OUT of a place (same person can be both, viewed from different places).
  • Push factors (repel from origin): unemployment, poor living conditions, political turmoil, natural disasters, epidemics.
  • Pull factors (attract to destination): better jobs, education, healthcare, security, pleasant climate.
  • Migration types: Permanent / Temporary / Seasonal; Rural-Rural, Rural-Urban, Urban-Urban, Urban-Rural.
💡 Mnemonic — Push vs Pull PuSH = SHoves you away (bad conditions at origin). PULL = pULLs you towards (good conditions at destination).

28. India's Population Growth Phases (1901–2011)

Part of Population Geography — very frequently tested timeline

PhasePeriodCharacteristic
Stationary/Stagnant1901–1921High birth & death rates cancel out; "Great Divide" — negative growth in 1911-21 due to influenza pandemic & famine
Steady Growth1921–1951Death rate begins declining (health/sanitation improve); birth rate stays high; 1921 = "Year of Great Divide"
Population Explosion1951–1981Death rate falls rapidly (medical advances); fertility remains high → sharp population rise
High Growth, Slowing1981–2011Growth rate begins declining gradually, but large absolute additions continue (e.g. 182 million added in the 1990s alone)
  • Growth rate trend: 2.22% (1971-81) → 2.14% (1981-91) → 1.93% (1991-2001) → 1.64% (2001-11, i.e. 17.64% decadal).
  • Total Fertility Rate declined from 5.2 (1971) to ~2.0 (recent) — nearing replacement level (2.1).
  • Population stabilization targeted by National Population Policy 2000, expected around 2045-2050.
💡 Mnemonic — 4 Phases "Stay Steady, then Explode Slowly" → Stationary → Steady growth → Explosion → Slowing growth.

🏛️ Land Reforms & Farming Systems

  • Land reforms (post-independence): abolition of intermediaries (Zamindari abolition), tenancy reforms, land ceiling acts, consolidation of holdings.
  • Intensive Agriculture District Programme (IADP) & Intensive Agriculture Area Programme (IAAP) — early Green Revolution-era schemes.
  • Shifting cultivation ("slash and burn"): practiced in NE India — locally called Jhum (NE India), Podu (Andhra/Odisha), Bewar/Dahiya (MP).
  • MSP (Minimum Support Price): government-guaranteed price for select crops, recommended by CACP (Commission for Agricultural Costs & Prices).
  • PM-KISAN, PM Fasal Bima Yojana, e-NAM — major current agriculture schemes.

🏭 Industrial Regions & Classification

  • Agro-based industries: cotton, sugar, jute, food processing (raw material dependent).
  • Mineral-based industries: iron & steel, cement, aluminium.
  • Major world industrial belts: North-East USA, Western/Central Europe (Ruhr), Eastern Asia (Japan/China/South Korea).
  • Kobe-Osaka region (Japan): classic industrial agglomeration example, cited for attracting dense population via employment.

🌐 Global Transport & Trade Networks

  • TEN-T (Trans-European Transport Network): EU's integrated network connecting roads, railways, airports & waterways across Europe — not limited to one mode.
  • Suez Canal: connects Mediterranean Sea to Red Sea — shortens Europe-Asia sea route (avoids going around Africa).
  • Panama Canal: connects Atlantic & Pacific Oceans, cutting through Central America.
  • Strait of Malacca: key chokepoint between Indian & Pacific Oceans (Indonesia-Malaysia).

🧭 Key Global Resource/Trade Facts

  • India accounts for a growing share of global services exports (IT/ITES) — Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Pune are major IT hubs.
  • HDI (Human Development Index): composite measure by UNDP combining health (life expectancy), education (schooling years), and standard of living (GNI per capita) — emphasizes human capability over pure economic growth.
  • Demographic Dividend countries (like India) have a resource advantage if skilling & job creation keep pace with the working-age population bulge.
