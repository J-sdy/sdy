📌 Mughal Empire – Overview
  • 300-year spectacle of wealth, warfare & breathtaking art
  • Founded by a fugitive prince from Central Asia
  • Blended Persian, Islamic & Indian traditions → unique "Indo-Islamic" civilization
🏹 BABUR 1526–1530
1st Mughal Emperor
Early Struggles (1494–1504)
  • At age 12, inherited principality of Farghana (modern-day Uzbekistan) after father Umar Sheikh Mirza died
  • Descendant of Timur (father's side) & Genghis Khan (mother's side)
  • Obsessed with capturing Samarkand – ancestral seat of Timur
  • Captured Samarkand 3 times but could never hold it → lost both Samarkand & Farghana → became a "homeless prince"
  • Kabul (1504): Moved south, captured Kabul → became base for India
Entry into India – Invited By
  • 1. Daulat Khan Lodi – Governor of Punjab
  • 2. Alam Khan – Uncle of Ibrahim Lodi
  • 3. Rana Sanga – King of Mewar (per Babur's memoirs)
  • ⚠️ They believed Babur would raid & leave like Timur — underestimated his intention to stay

Military Techniques of Babur
  • Tulughma System – encirclement/flanking tactic
  • Araba (Wagon-Fort) Method – cannons hidden behind wagons; Ottoman influence
  • Mobility & Horse Archers – Parthian shot, kiting, rapid attack (Turco-Mongol style)
  • Gunpowder & Field Artillery – light & movable; matchlockmen; cannon fire-inducing sounds
  • Discipline & Terrain Use – disciplined formations vs terrain anchorage, fortified ditches
Literature
  • Tuzuk-i-Baburi (Baburnama) – written in Chagatai Turkic; candid autobiography
  • Translated into Persian by Abdur Rahim Khan-i-Khana in 1589 during Akbar's reign
🧠 Babur's 4 Battles in Order
"Panipat's Khan Charged Gallantly" → Panipat · Khanwa · Chanderi · Ghagra
😔 HUMAYUN 1530–1540 & 1555–1556
2nd Mughal Emperor
📜 Famous Quote
"Humayun tumbled through life, and he tumbled out of it." – Historian Stanley Lane-Poole
Early Challenges – 3 Main Enemies
  • 1. Afghans – led by brilliant Sher Shah Suri in East (Bihar/Bengal)
  • 2. Bahadur Shah of Gujarat – powerful regional ruler from West
  • 3. His Own Brothers – Kamran, Hindal & Askari frequently revolted
  • Siege of Chunar (1532): First encounter with Sher Shah; Humayun accepted tactical submission → gave Sher Shah time to consolidate
Conflict with Sher Shah Suri
  • Battle of Chausa (1539): Sher Shah surprise attack; Humayun escaped by jumping into Ganges, saved by water-carrier Nizam
  • Battle of Kannauj/Bilgram (1540): Decisive defeat; Humayun lost throne & fled
Period of Exile (1540–1555) – 15 Years
  • Wandered through Rajasthan, Sindh, eventually Persia
  • Birth of Akbar (1542) – born in exile at Amarkot (Umarkot) in palace of Rana Prasad
  • Safavid Help: Took refuge with Shah Tahmasp of Persia; agreed to convert to Shia Islam (nominally) & cede Kandahar to Persians in exchange for military aid
Restoration & Death (1555–1556)
  • After Sher Shah's death & decline of Sur Dynasty → opportunity arose
  • With loyal general Bairam Khan → defeated Afghans at Battle of Machhiwara & Sirhind (1555)
  • Re-occupied Delhi in July 1555
  • Tragic End (1556): Died falling from stairs of his library, Sher Mandal, at Purana Qila
Art & Literature
  • During exile recruited Persian painters Mir Sayyid Ali & Abdus Samad → laid foundation for Mughal School of Painting
  • Sister Gulbadan Begum wrote Humayun-Nama – rare intimate look into Mughal royal household
🔶 Sher Shah Suri (1486–1545) – The Interregnum
Not a Mughal – Sur Empire
Rise to Power
  • Born as Farid Khan in Sasaram, Bihar; served in Bihar Sultan's army
  • Captured Sasaram; established Sur Empire as ruler of North India
Key Achievements
  • Introduced uniform land revenue measurement; land measured in Bigha & classified on soil basis
  • Average produce calculated; 1/3rd was state's share
  • Revenue collected in cash, not in kind
  • Peasants given receipts (Patta); state had records (Qabuliyat)
  • Reduced role of middlemen; fair treatment of peasants
Administrative Reforms
  • Centralized administration with well-organized bureaucracy
  • Divided empire into Provinces (Sarkar), Districts (Pargana) & Villages
  • Officials appointed on merit basis; efficient espionage system; comprehensive revenue system
Economic Reforms
  • Uniform system of weights & measures; encouraged trade & commerce
  • Issued silver coins (Rupee) of high purity → became the standard
  • Promoted agriculture & irrigation
Public Welfare
  • Built Grand Trunk Road (Bengal to Punjab) – most famous road
  • Planted trees on both sides; built rest houses (Sarai) for travelers
  • Constructed wells, bridges; ensured facilities for people
Military & Other
  • Strong disciplined army; used artillery effectively
  • Built many forts, mosques & Sarai
  • His tomb in Sasaram – fine example of Indo-Islamic architecture
  • Known for justice, discipline & concern for subjects
💡 Key Line
"Sher Shah Suri's reforms laid a strong foundation for good governance, which was later followed by the Mughals."
⚔️ AKBAR 1556–1605
3rd Mughal Emperor – "Empire Builder"
Early Phase: Regency & Consolidation
  • Accession (1556): Crowned at age 13 at Kalanaur (Punjab); tutor Bairam Khan appointed Regent (Wakil)
  • Second Battle of Panipat (1556): Bairam Khan defeated Hemu (general of Sur dynasty) → "real" start of Mughal Empire
  • End of Regency (1560–62): Dismissed Bairam Khan; survived "Petticoat Government" (1560–62) where foster mother Maham Anga held significant influence
Rajput Policy – A Masterstroke
  • Realized to rule India → needed support of Rajputs, most powerful local warriors
  • Matrimonial Alliances (1562): Married daughter of Raja Bharmal of Amber (Harka Bai / Jodha Bai) → Amber house became most loyal allies
  • Watan Jagir: Allowed Rajput kings to keep ancestral lands (Watan) as long as they accepted Mughal suzerainty
  • Battle of Haldighati (1576): Maharana Pratap of Mewar resisted; Akbar's army led by Raja Man Singh (a Rajput!) defeated him; Mewar never fully absorbed

⚙️ Akbar's Administrative Reforms
A. Mansabdari System
  • Unique grading system for all civil & military officers
  • Zat: Personal rank & salary
  • Sawar: Number of cavalrymen the officer had to maintain
  • Dagh: Branding of horses (to prevent fraud)
  • Chehra: Descriptive rolls of soldiers
  • Ranks ranged from 10 to 10,000
B. Land Revenue – Zabt / Dahshala System
  • Created by Raja Todar Mal (Navaratna)
  • Dahshala: Average produce & prices of last 10 years; state's share = 1/3rd
  • Land Classification: Polaj (best, always cultivated) → Parauti → Chachar → Banjar (worst)
🧠 Land Classification (Best → Worst)
"Poor Peasants Can't Bear" → Polaj · Parauti · Chachar · Banjar
🕌 Akbar's Religious Policy – Sulh-i-Kul

Shifted from orthodox Islam to "Universal Peace"

Timeline of Religious Reforms
  • 1563: Abolished Pilgrim Tax
  • 1564: Abolished Jizya (poll tax on non-Muslims)
  • 1575: Built Ibadat Khana (House of Worship) at Fatehpur Sikri
  • 1579: Issued Mazhar-i-Nama (Infallibility Decree) – made him supreme judge in religious matters
  • 1582: Introduced Din-i-Ilahi (Tawhid-i-Ilahi) – NOT a religion; a code of conduct; Birbal was the only prominent noble to accept it
💡 Year Trick for Akbar's Reforms
63-64-75-79-82: "63 is pilgrim year, 64 Jizya's fear, 75 built worship's peer, 79 Mazhar's decree was clear, 82 Din-i-Ilahi appeared"

🎨 JAHANGIR 1605–1627
4th Mughal Emperor – "Empire Preserver"
  • Born as Prince Salim; remembered as "Empire Preserver" & great patron of arts
  • Title: Nur-ud-din Muhammad Jahangir (Conqueror of the World) – taken 1605
Early Challenges
  • Rebellion of Prince Khusrau: Immediately after accession; his eldest son Khusrau rebelled with help of 5th Sikh Guru, Guru Arjan Dev
  • Consequence: Jahangir executed Guru Arjan Dev → beginning of long-standing conflict between Mughals & Sikhs
Major Military Conquests
  • Mewar (1615): Settlement with Amar Singh (son of Maharana Pratap); Ranas accepted Mughal suzerainty but exempted from attending court personally
  • Deccan (Ahmednagar): Sent Prince Khurram (later Shah Jahan); success earned Khurram title "Shah Jahan"; but Malik Ambar (PM of Ahmednagar) prevented total takeover using guerrilla warfare
  • Kangra (1620): Successfully captured strategic Kangra Fort in Himalayan foothills
The Era of Nur Jahan
  • 1611: Married Mehr-un-Nissa, titled Nur Jahan (Light of the World)
  • Only Mughal Empress to have coins minted in her name & issue royal farmans (decrees)
  • Nur Jahan Junta: Formed powerful group with her father (Itimad-ud-Daula), brother (Asaf Khan) & Prince Khurram → dominated court for nearly a decade
  • Later rift between Nur Jahan & Shah Jahan → Shah Jahan's rebellion → weakened empire in final years
Administration & Justice
  • Zanjir-i-Adal (Chain of Justice): Golden chain with 60 bells at Agra Fort; any subject could pull for direct audience with Emperor
  • 12 Edicts (Dastur-ul-Amal): Upon accession; banned cutting noses/ears as punishment; prohibited forced sale of land
Europeans in Jahangir's Court
VisitorYearKey Fact
William Hawkins1608Arrived with letter from King James I; given title "English Khan"; no trade concessions due to Portuguese pressure
Sir Thomas Roe1615Official ambassador of British East India Company; successfully secured permission to trade & establish factories at Surat
Art, Culture & Literature
  • Jahangir's reign = Golden Age of Mughal Painting
  • Shifted from manuscript illustrations to individual portraits & detailed nature studies (birds, animals, flowers)
  • Famous painters: Ustad Mansur (flora & fauna) & Abul Hasan
  • Literature: Tuzuk-i-Jahangiri – autobiography in Persian; noted for frankness & scientific observations of nature
🏗️ SHAH JAHAN 1628–1658
5th Mughal Emperor – "Engineer King"
  • Born as Prince Khurram; known as "Engineer King"
  • Reign = "Golden Age" of Mughal architecture but also start of financial strain
Accession & Early Challenges
  • Ascended throne in 1628 with support of father-in-law Asaf Khan
  • Title: Shah Jahan (King of the World)
  • Bundela Rebellion: Led by Jujhar Singh – first major challenge
  • Khan-i-Jahan Lodi: Afghan noble who rebelled; defeated & killed in 1631
Military Conquests & Deccan Policy
  • Annexation of Ahmadnagar (1633): Ended independence of Nizam Shahi dynasty
  • Treaties with Bijapur & Golconda (1636): Forced to accept Mughal suzerainty & pay tribute; not fully annexed
  • Central Asian Campaign: Attempted to reclaim Balkh & Badakhshan → major failure; massive loss of men & money
  • Loss of Kandahar (1649): Persians permanently seized Kandahar → blow to prestige & strategic defense
Administration & Revenue
  • Month-Scale System: "Monthly Ratio" in Mansabdari; if Jagir didn't yield expected revenue → adjusted to 10-month, 8-month, or 6-month scale
  • State demand raised to 50% of produce in some regions to fund buildings → increased peasant burden
Literature & Culture
  • Dara Shikoh's Work: Translated Upanishads into Persian as Sirr-i-Akbar (The Great Secret); wrote Majma-ul-Bahrayn (The Mingling of Two Oceans) – exploring commonalities between Islam & Hinduism
  • Other Dara Shikoh works: Safain-ul-Auliya, Hasanat-ul-Arifeen, Risala-i-Haqqnuma, Tarjuma-i-Yogvashishtha
  • Court Historian: Abdul Hamid Lahori wrote Padshahnama – official history of Shah Jahan's reign
War of Succession (1657–1658)
  • When Shah Jahan fell ill → brutal civil war between 4 sons:
  • 1. Dara Shikoh – eldest; scholar of Upanishads; Emperor's favorite
  • 2. Shuja – Governor of Bengal
  • 3. Aurangzeb – Governor of Deccan; seasoned military general
  • 4. Murad – Governor of Gujarat
  • Aurangzeb won → defeated Dara Shikoh at Battle of Samugarh (1658)
  • Imprisoned Shah Jahan in Agra Fort for remaining 8 years of his life
💡 Dara Shikoh's Famous Quote
"The different religions are but different paths leading to the same God."
🕌 AURANGZEB 1658–1707
6th Mughal Emperor – Last "Great Mughal"
  • Title: Alamgir ("Conqueror of the World")
  • Last of the "Great Mughals"; reigned for 49 years
  • Empire reached maximum territorial extent but also sowed seeds of decline
Character & Personal Life
  • Lived a very simple, austere life unlike predecessors
  • Called Zinda Pir (Living Saint) for disciplined habits & avoidance of royal luxuries
  • Secured throne after bloody civil war, defeating brothers Shuja, Murad & crown prince Dara Shikoh
Two Phases of Reign
Phase 1 (1658–1681)
Focused on Northern India – dealing with Jats, Satnamis & Sikhs
Phase 2 (1681–1707)
Focused entirely on Deccan; never returned to Delhi
Territorial Extent
  • Empire stretched from Kabul to Chittagong and from Kashmir to Kaveri River
Major Military Campaigns
  • North-East: General Mir Jumla annexed Assam (Ahom kingdom) in 1662; Mughals struggled to maintain control due to geography
  • Annexed Bijapur (1686) & Golconda (1687)
  • Removing these kingdoms eliminated the "buffer" between Mughals & Marathas → direct exhausting conflict
  • Conflict with Marathas: Rise of Shivaji Maharaj = biggest challenge
  • Despite Treaty of Purandar (1665), Shivaji escaped Mughal custody in Agra & established sovereign Maratha kingdom
  • Aurangzeb spent last 25 years in Deccan trying to crush Marathas but failed
⚠️ Religious & Social Policies of Aurangzeb

Moved away from Akbar's Sulh-i-Kul toward more orthodox Islamic state

  • Jizya (1679): Re-introduced Jizya tax on non-Muslims (abolished by Akbar 115 years earlier)
  • Muhtasibs: Appointed censors of public morals to ensure people lived per Sharia law
  • Banned music, gambling & alcohol in court
  • Temple Policy: Issued some grants to temples but also ordered destruction of prominent ones (Vishwanath in Kashi, Keshavdeva in Mathura) – primarily for political reasons/punishing rebellions
  • Sikh Conflict: Executed 9th Sikh Guru, Guru Tegh Bahadur (1675) after he refused to convert → led to permanent militarization of Sikhs under Guru Gobind Singh (The Khalsa)
⚠️ Exam Note – Sikh Gurus Conflict
Guru Arjan Dev (5th) → executed by Jahangir
Guru Tegh Bahadur (9th) → executed by Aurangzeb (1675) → led to formation of Khalsa
📉 Administration & Decline
Jagirdari Crisis
  • Empire so large → not enough good land (Jagirs) for increasing Mansabdars
  • Led to internal corruption & competition
The Deccan Ulcer
  • "The Deccan ulcer ruined Aurangzeb" – historians' verdict
  • 26-year-long campaign drained royal treasury, exhausted army
  • Administrative collapse in Northern India due to Emperor's long absence
Literature
  • Fatawa-e-Alamgiri – massive compilation of Islamic law; created under his patronage; served as legal code for the empire

🏛️ Mughal Architecture – Overview (1526–1707)
  • Represents the peak of Indo-Islamic architecture in India
  • Fusion of Persian, Timurid (Central Asian), Islamic & indigenous Indian styles
  • Known for symmetry, monumentality, gardens, domes, arches & decorative elegance
  • Evolved continuously from Babur to Aurangzeb

🏗️ 19 Elements & Features of Mughal Architecture
1. Arches
Curved structural openings above doors/windows; Mughals used pointed & cusped arches with voussoirs & keystone
2. Domes
Hemispherical structures covering large spaces; Mughal domes are usually bulbous & set on a drum
3. Double Dome
Two domes – inner for interior proportion, outer for external height & magnificence
4. Minarets
Tall slender towers at corners of platform; add vertical emphasis; used for call to prayer
5. Charbagh
Persian-style four-part garden divided by water channels; symbolizing paradise as in Quran
6. Pietra Dura (Parchin Kari)
Art of inlaying semi-precious stones into marble to create floral & geometric patterns
7. Jali Work
Perforated stone lattice screens with intricate geometric/floral patterns; let light & air while maintaining privacy
8. Calligraphy
Quranic verses inscribed using calligraphic scripts like Thuluth & Naskh
9. Chhatris
Small elevated dome-shaped kiosks; adopted from Rajput architecture
10. Jharokhas
Overhanging enclosed balconies projecting from walls; derived from Rajput architecture
11. Materials
Red sandstone (Akbar era) → White marble (Shah Jahan era)
12. Symmetry
Buildings planned with perfect symmetry along central axis; reflecting balance, order & imperial authority
13. Iwan
Vaulted hall/space open on one side; typical Persian architectural element adopted by Mughals
14. Pishtaq
Tall rectangular framed archway used as grand entrance; creates monumental impression
15. Arabesque
Intricate floral, geometric & vegetal patterns used as decoration
16. Muqarnas
Honeycomb-like decorative vaulting used in arches, domes & ceilings
17. Finial
Ornamental element at top of domes or minarets
18. Hasht Bihisht
Persian planning concept – central chamber surrounded by eight rooms ("Eight Paradise Plan")
19. Water Channels & Fountains
Used in gardens for beauty, cooling & symbolic representation of paradise
🏡 Babur's Architecture 1526–1530
  • Style: Persian-Central Asian Garden Style; simple but deeply influenced by Persian & Central Asian traditions
  • Introduced concept of Charbagh (four-part garden) divided by water channels in India
  • Flowing water, tanks & fountains used for beauty, cooling & paradise symbolism
Major Buildings
  • Aram Bagh, Agra – first Mughal garden in India; Charbagh style; had pavilions, fruit trees, flower beds
  • Kabuli Bagh Mosque, Panipat – built after First Battle of Panipat (1526); one of earliest Mughal mosques; Persian arched openings; brick & stone
  • Jama Masjid, Sambhal – one of earliest mosques; reflects early Persian influence; built for local Muslim community
🏯 Humayun's Architecture 1530–1556
  • Style: Strong Persianized Mughal Style; moved away from Afghan simplicity
  • Key features: Iwans & Pishtaqs, Charbagh gardens, Double Dome System, Symmetry & Axial Planning, Chhatris & Jaalis
Major Buildings
  • Dinpanah (Delhi) – new city built by Humayun in 1533; named "Abode of Faith"; later captured by Sher Shah; renamed Purana Qila; contains Qila-i-Kuhna Mosque (1541) & Sher Mandal
  • Humayun's Tomb (1570) – built by his widow Begha Begum; architect Mirak Mirza Ghiyas; first grand Mughal tomb in India & UNESCO World Heritage Site; first major use of double dome; Charbagh layout; red sandstone + white marble; prototype for Taj Mahal; Hasht Bihisht plan
🔶 Sher Shah Suri's Architecture 1540–1545
  • Style: Transitional Indo-Afghan; bridge between Lodi simplicity & Mughal grandeur
  • Massive, strong & functional structures; use of red sandstone & rubble masonry
  • Minimal ornamentation; focus on durability; octagonal tomb style
Major Buildings
  • Sher Shah's Tomb, Sasaram – on an artificial lake; octagonal plan on raised platform; red sandstone with white marble inlay; chhatris on corners; blend of strength, beauty & serenity
  • Purana Qila (Delhi) – expanded Humayun's Dinpanah; includes Qila-i-Kuhna Mosque & Sher Mandal (library)
  • Rohtas Fort (Punjab, Pakistan) – massive hill fort; thick walls, gateways & watchtowers; designed to withstand long sieges
🔴 Akbar's Architecture 1556–1605
  • Style: "The Great Synthesizer" – fusion of Persian, Central Asian, Hindu, Jain & Buddhist traditions
  • Key features: Red sandstone construction, Trabeate style (pillars & beams), Chhatris, Jharokhas, Brackets & Corbels, Jali work, Lotus motifs
  • Massive & bold structures; incorporated Indian elements extensively
Major Buildings
  • Agra Fort (c. 1565) – first major architectural venture; red sandstone; semi-circular plan; strong fortifications; palaces, audience halls, mosques
  • Fatehpur Sikri (1571–1585) – imperial capital built to honor Sheikh Salim Chishti; planned city; architectural fusion:
    • Buland Darwaza (1601) – after Gujarat victory; largest gateway in India (55m high)
    • Panch Mahal – 5-storey palace inspired by Buddhist vihara
    • Diwan-i-Khas – famous for central pillar with radiating platforms
    • Ibadat Khana – hall for interfaith discussions
    • Tomb of Salim Chishti – originally red sandstone, later rebuilt in marble; exquisite jali work
    • Jodha Bai Palace – strong Rajput influence with chhatris, brackets & carved pillars
  • Akbar's Tomb, Sikandra (completed by Jahangir) – blend of Islamic & Hindu elements; no central dome; pyramidal look; red sandstone & marble
  • Other: Lahore Fort (early), Allahabad Fort, Ajmer Fort & Mosque, Sarai & Caravanserais
🤍 Jahangir's Architecture 1605–1627
  • Style: "Patron of Refined Beauty & Nature"; shift from massive red sandstone → white marble
  • Key features: Use of white marble, Pietra Dura work, Gardens & Charbagh, Delicate ornamentation, Harmony with nature
Major Buildings
  • Itmad-ud-Daulah Tomb, Agra – built by Nur Jahan for her father; called "Baby Taj"; first Mughal structure entirely of white marble; first extensive use of pietra dura; prototype for later marble architecture including Taj Mahal
  • Shalimar Bagh, Srinagar (Kashmir) – magnificent Charbagh garden along Dal Lake; terraced layout, Chinar trees, water channels, fountains, pavilions
  • Akbar's Tomb, Sikandra – completed during Jahangir's reign
  • Jahangir's Palaces at Lahore Fort – Khwabgah (Dream Palace), Diwan-i-Khas, Sheesh Mahal; fine frescoes, painted ceilings, mirror work
  • Other: Sarai Nurmahal (caravanserai), Nishat Bagh/Chashme Shahi (Kashmir)
⭐ Shah Jahan's Architecture 1628–1658
  • Style: "Zenith of Mughal Architecture"; dominant use of white marble with red sandstone accents
  • Key features: White marble, Pietra Dura inlay, Perfect symmetry, Bulbous domes & slender minarets, Calligraphy, Charbagh gardens
Major Buildings
  • Taj Mahal, Agra (1632–1653) – built in memory of wife Mumtaz Mahal; pinnacle of Mughal architecture; pure white marble; perfect symmetry; pietra dura inlay; Charbagh on raised platform; double dome & 4 minarets
  • Red Fort (Lal Qila), Delhi (1639–1648) – new imperial capital Shahjahanabad; red sandstone with marble accents; Diwan-i-Am, Diwan-i-Khas, Rang Mahal, Khas Mahal, Sheesh Mahal; beautiful marble pavilions & gardens
  • Jama Masjid, Delhi (1650–1656) – one of largest & most magnificent mosques; huge courtyard; 3 large marble domes & 2 tall minarets; red sandstone & marble
  • Musamman Burj, Agra Fort (1631) – octagonal marble tower where Shah Jahan was imprisoned by Aurangzeb; overlooks Taj Mahal
  • Moti Masjid, Agra Fort (1648–1654) – entirely white marble; 3 domes & delicate arches; "Pearl Mosque"
  • Other: Shalimar Bagh (Lahore), Khas Mahal (Agra Fort), Jahanaras Sarai (Delhi), Takht-i-Taus (Peacock Throne) – legendary throne symbolizing imperial grandeur
📉 Aurangzeb's Architecture 1658–1707
  • Style: Declining & Simplified; shift toward simplicity, functionality & piety
  • Reduced ornamentation; less white marble; austere & economical structures
  • Greater emphasis on mosques, religious buildings, forts & practical constructions
Major Buildings
  • Bibi Ka Maqbara, Aurangabad (c. 1661–1678) – built in memory of wife Dilras Banu Begum (Rabia-ud-Daurani); called "Taj of Deccan"; resembles Taj Mahal on smaller scale; marble with minimal pietra dura
  • Badshahi Mosque, Lahore (1671–1673) – one of largest mosques in world; red sandstone with marble inlay; 3 large marble domes; 4 minarets; vast prayer hall
  • Alamgir Masjid, Delhi (c. 1669) – built on raised platform near Red Fort; small & simple compared to earlier mosques
  • Makki Masjid, Delhi (c. 1660) – near Jama Masjid; red sandstone; simple yet elegant
  • Daulatabad Fort – strengthened & renovated; important military stronghold in Deccan
  • Other: Built Sarais, Bridges & Roads, Fortifications (Gwalior, Ranthambore, Jinj), Water Tanks & Wells
  • Religious & Educational: Built Madrasas, renovated Mazars & Dargahs, built many mosques; supported Khanqas (Sufi institutions)

⚡ QUICK REVISION – Mughal Empire
  • Founded: 1526 by Babur; ended: 1857
  • Babur: Farghana → Kabul → India; 4 battles: Panipat, Khanwa, Chanderi, Ghagra
  • Humayun: Lost to Sher Shah Suri; Akbar born at Amarkot (1542); died falling from Sher Mandal (1556)
  • Akbar: Crowned at 13 at Kalanaur; Real start = 2nd Battle of Panipat (1556); 9 Navaratnas; Din-i-Ilahi (1582)
  • Jahangir: Chain of Justice; Nur Jahan most powerful empress; Golden Age of Mughal Painting
  • Shah Jahan: "Engineer King"; Taj Mahal (1632–53); War of Succession → Aurangzeb won (1658)
  • Aurangzeb: "Alamgir"; 49-year reign; Deccan Ulcer; Jagirdari Crisis; re-imposed Jizya (1679)

🎯 Tricky Exam Facts

  • 💡 Akbar crowned at Kalanaur, Punjab (NOT Delhi)
  • 💡 Second Battle of Panipat (1556) = "real" start of Mughal Empire (not 1526!)
  • 💡 Akbar's army at Haldighati was led by a Rajput (Raja Man Singh) – not Akbar himself
  • 💡 Birbal = only prominent noble to accept Din-i-Ilahi
  • 💡 Itmad-ud-Daulah = first marble tomb (Jahangir), NOT Taj Mahal
  • 💡 Humayun's Tomb = first double dome & prototype for Taj Mahal
  • 💡 Taj Mahal architect = Ustad Ahmad Lahauri (generally credited)
  • 💡 Nur Jahan = only Mughal Empress to mint coins in her name
  • 💡 Buland Darwaza (55m) = largest gateway in India; built after Gujarat victory
  • 💡 Guru Arjan Dev (5th Guru) → Jahangir | Guru Tegh Bahadur (9th Guru) → Aurangzeb
  • 💡 Panch Mahal at Fatehpur Sikri inspired by Buddhist Vihara style
  • 💡 Fatawa-e-Alamgiri = Islamic law compilation under Aurangzeb's patronage
  • 💡 Dara Shikoh = highest mansab ever: 60,000 Zat & 40,000 Sawar
  • 💡 Bibi Ka Maqbara = "Taj of Deccan"; Aurangzeb built for his wife; reflects decline phase
  • 💡 Sher Shah Suri's Rupee = became the standard silver coin (even followed by Mughals)
  • 💡 Grand Trunk Road = Sher Shah Suri's contribution; Bengal to Punjab
  • 💡 Mughal School of Painting foundation = Persian painters Mir Sayyid Ali & Abdus Samad (recruited by Humayun in exile)
